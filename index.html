Sourdough Pulse Rolls

Sourdough Pulse Rolls

Ingredients

  • Sourdough Starter (I usually use about a cup)
  • 7 cups of flour (does not really matter, as long as it suitable for bread)
  • Water as needed (about 5-6 cups)
  • 2 cups of pulses
  • Extra flour to adjust dough consistency

Instructions

  1. Prepare the sourdough by mixing the starter and the flour and water. I like to expand over 3 days, by adding 1 cup of flour on day 1, 2 cups on day 2, and 3 cups on day 3 and slowly increasing thickness of the dough.
  2. Save about one cup of the dough on Day 3 as starter for the next batch (you can freeze it, in the fridge it is good for about 2 weeks).
  3. On Day 4: finish the dough adding flour to get a stiff, non-tacky consistency, form it into the desired shape, let it rest for 1-2 hours at room temperature.
  4. Bake for 30-45 minutes at 450 degrees.

Spicy Black Bean Tacos

Spicy Black Bean Tacos

Ingredients

  • 2 cans black beans, drained and rinsed
  • 8 corn tortillas
  • 1 avocado, sliced
  • 1 cup shredded cabbage
  • 1/2 cup cilantro, chopped
  • 1 lime, cut into wedges
  • 1 tsp cumin
  • 1 tsp chili powder
  • Hot sauce to taste

Instructions

  1. Heat black beans with cumin and chili powder in a pan.
  2. Warm tortillas in a dry skillet or microwave.
  3. Mash half the beans for texture, leave others whole.
  4. Fill tortillas with bean mixture.
  5. Top with avocado, cabbage, cilantro, and hot sauce.
  6. Serve with lime wedges.

Introduction to Pulses

Types of Pulses

There's a wide variety of pulses available, each with their unique flavor profiles, textures, and culinary applications. Here are some common types:

Beans

Including black beans, kidney beans, navy beans, pinto beans, and more. Beans are versatile and can be used in soups, stews, salads, and as side dishes.

Lentils

Including red, green, brown, and black lentils. They cook quickly and are perfect for soups, stews, salads, and as a meat substitute in many dishes.

Chickpeas

Also known as garbanzo beans, chickpeas are used in hummus, falafels, curries, and salads. They have a nutty flavor and creamy texture.

Dry Peas

Including yellow and green split peas. They're commonly used in soups, stews, and purees, and are especially popular in winter recipes.

Nutritional Benefits of Pulses

Pulses are nutritional powerhouses that offer numerous health benefits. Including them regularly in your diet can contribute significantly to your overall health and wellbeing.

Protein-Rich

Pulses are an excellent source of plant-based protein, containing about 20-25% protein by weight. They're particularly valuable for vegetarians and vegans as a meat alternative.

High in Fiber

With both soluble and insoluble fiber, pulses help maintain digestive health, regulate blood sugar levels, and promote a feeling of fullness that can aid in weight management.

Low Glycemic Index

Pulses have a low glycemic index, meaning they cause a slower, more gradual rise in blood sugar levels, making them suitable for people with diabetes or insulin resistance.

Rich in Micronutrients

Pulses provide essential vitamins and minerals including folate, iron, potassium, magnesium, and zinc, contributing to overall nutritional adequacy.

Heart-Healthy

Regular consumption of pulses has been associated with reduced risk of heart disease, partly due to their cholesterol-lowering effects and blood pressure regulation.

Sustainable Choice

Pulses require less water to grow than many other protein sources and can fix nitrogen in the soil, reducing the need for synthetic fertilizers.

Incorporating Pulses into Your Diet

Adding pulses to your daily meals doesn't have to be complicated. Here are practical ways to include these nutritional powerhouses in your diet:

Start Small

If you're new to pulses, start by incorporating small amounts into familiar dishes. Add a half cup of lentils to your spaghetti sauce or mix some black beans into your favorite salsa.

Use Canned Options

For convenience, keep canned beans and lentils in your pantry. Just drain and rinse them to reduce sodium content before using.

Batch Cook

Cook a large batch of pulses at the beginning of the week and refrigerate or freeze in portion-sized containers for quick additions to meals throughout the week.

Blend Into Smoothies

White beans and chickpeas can be blended into fruit smoothies for added protein and creaminess without altering the flavor significantly.

Make Plant-Based Swaps

Use pulses as a substitute for meat in traditional recipes. Lentil bolognese, chickpea "tuna" salad, and black bean burgers are all delicious options.

Explore Global Cuisines

Many traditional diets worldwide feature pulses prominently. Try Indian dal, Mexican beans, Middle Eastern hummus, or Italian pasta e fagioli for inspiration.

Pulses: Effects Of Increasing Pulse Consumption On Cardiovascular Disease Risk Factors

Study Purpose

    The purpose of this USDA funded trial is to assess the effects of consuming two doses of pulses (1.5 cups/wk and 3 cups/wk) on:

    • Evaluate comprehensive cardiovascular health markers, including critical blood lipid profiles, arterial blood pressure dynamics, and glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) levels ‚Äì key indicators of long-term metabolic health and cardiovascular resilience
    • Assess transformative changes in nutritional excellence through detailed analysis of dietary patterns, nutrient density, and overall nutritional optimization
    • Investigate the profound relationship between pulse consumption and quality of life metrics, examining how these nutrient-rich foods influence both psychological well-being and gastronomic satisfaction in adult participants

    Public Health Context

    • 60% of U.S. adults live with at least one chronic disease, and 40% have two or more diet-related chronic conditions
    • Current dietary patterns contribute significantly to this health burden

    Pulses as a Nutritional Intervention

    • Pulses (beans, peas, lentils, chickpeas) are nutrient-dense foods featured in many healthy dietary patterns
    • Key nutritional characteristics: high fiber, low glycemic index, high protein, low fat, and rich in bioactive compounds
    • Current Dietary Guidelines for Americans recommend 1.5 cup-equivalents of pulses per week
    • Evidence suggests increasing to 3 cup-equivalents weekly may provide additional health benefits

    Research Gap

    • While studies show regular pulse consumption can reduce cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk factors, the optimal intake amount remains unclear
    • This study aims to determine if there is justification to increase the DGA recommendation for pulses

Frequently Asked Questions About Our Research

When will the next cohort begin? ‚ñº
Recruitment begins following IRB approval and continues until 180 subjects are enrolled. The next cohort is expected to start within 2-3 months of current study completion.
Are previous participants able to participate again? ‚ñº
No, previous participants are excluded to avoid carryover effects and maintain study integrity. Each participant can only take part in the study once.
How can participants spread word of future studies? ‚ñº
Participants can refer friends and family through word-of-mouth, share study flyers from Tucson Village Farms, or provide contact information to the research team for future recruitment. We appreciate your help in spreading awareness!
Will participants receive individualized results of their lab work? ‚ñº
No, participants will not receive their individual lab results (blood lipids, blood pressure, HbA1c).
Where can participants access the general/full results of each study? ‚ñº
General study results will be posted on the university research website and shared via email newsletter to all participants within 6 months of study completion.
How will participants' information be disposed of following the study's conclusion? ‚ñº
All participant data will be securely destroyed 7 years after study completion per university policy. Participants can request immediate data deletion at any time.
Who is eligible to participate? ‚ñº
Participants must be aged 18-65, and free from major chronic diseases. Specific inclusion and exclusion criteria will be reviewed during the screening process.
What does participation involve? ‚ñº
Participation involves 6 bi-weekly cooking sessions (1 hr each with TVF), two visits (Baseline and Endpoint) to our research lab center (fasting blood draw & Questionaires), completing weekly food logs, and providing blood samples for lab analysis.
What are the potential risks? ‚ñº
Risks are minimal and primarily related to blood draws (slight discomfort, bruising). Some participants may experience caffeine-related effects like jitters or sleep disturbance. All risks will be thoroughly discussed during consent.
Can I withdraw from the study? ‚ñº
Yes, participation is completely voluntary and you can withdraw at any time without penalty. Your decision will not affect any future medical care or benefits.
How is participant privacy protected? ‚ñº
All data is de-identified and stored securely. Only authorized research staff have access to participant information. The study follows all HIPAA guidelines and university privacy policies.
